Día de Los Muertos (Day of the Dead)

Once a year, our departed come back to celebrate with us

Description

Día de los Muertos (Day of the Dead) is a Mexican holiday that is mainly celebrated for two days, but some traditions and preparations may extend to include October 27th and November 3rd. Día de Los Muertos is known for the bright cempasuchil (marigold) flowers. Their vibrant color and scent are believed to guide the spirits back to the world of the living. This celebration is time for families to honor and remember their deceased loved ones. The celebration includes building altars (ofrendas) decorated with photos, marigold flowers, candles, and favorite foods of the departed. People also visit cemeteries to clean and decorate graves, often spending the night there in a festive atmosphere with music and food. Day of the Dead is a holiday for celebrating death and life. It is unlike any other holiday where the living and the dead come together to celebrate the memories of those who have passed away.

Celebration Activities

  • Building and decorating altars (ofrendas) with photos, marigold flowers, candles, and favorite foods of the departed.
  • Visiting cemeteries to clean and decorate graves, often spending the night there in a festive atmosphere with music and food.
  • Preparing traditional foods such as pan de muerto (a special bread), sugar skulls, and tamales.
  • Participating in parades and public celebrations featuring music, dancing, and colorful costumes.
  • Sharing stories and memories of deceased loved ones to keep their spirits alive.

Symbols of Día de los Muertos

Some of the main items and elements that need to be included in this celebration. These elements are used to create the ofrendas (altars) and to decorate gravesites.

  1. Cempasuchil (Marigold Flowers): Their vibrant color and scent are believed to guide the spirits back to the world of the living.
  2. Ofrendas (Altars): Decorated with photos, marigold flowers, candles, and favorite foods of the departed.
  3. Calaveras (Sugar Skulls): Represent the deceased and are often decorated with colorful icing and designs.
  4. Pan de Muerto: A special bread baked for the occasion, often shaped like a skull or crossbones.
  5. Candles: Used to light the way for the spirits to return.

The tradition of Día de los Muertos has roots that go back thousands of years to indigenous practices in Mexico, particularly among the Aztec civilization. The Aztecs had a month-long celebration dedicated to the goddess Mictecacihuatl, known as the "Lady of the Dead," which honored deceased ancestors. This celebration took place in the ninth month of the Aztec calendar, roughly corresponding to early August in the Gregorian calendar. When Spanish colonizers arrived in the 16th century, they brought with them Catholic traditions, including All Saints' Day and All Souls' Day. Over time, these indigenous and Catholic practices blended together to form the modern Día de los Muertos celebration, which is now observed on November 1st and 2nd.
